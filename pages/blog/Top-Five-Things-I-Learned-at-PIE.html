Top 5 Things I Learned at PIE

by Alyssa Carrizales

Nov. 11, 2017

"Your journey isn’t going to be smooth sailing. You’re going to encounter roadblocks that you can’t solve... "

Five things I learned at the Portland Incubator Experiment (PIE) that could benefit
any early-stage startup:


  1. Don't wait to execute.

    The PIE space held very little art. This may be why I remember one piece of art so clearly. It was two sentences painted hurriedly onto a white background - “Move fast. Break things.” This was the PIE mantra. This was the comment made to any startup founder that was getting stuck in the details. This was the answer to getting from idea to execution. My first lesson - if you have an idea for a company or project you think could be successful, don’t wait to execute. Build it. Test it. Break it. Repeat until you know whether or not there’s a viable market.


  2. Don’t skip the mission statement. Don’t skip the company values.

    The mission statement and company values are key elements in defining your company’s voice, messaging, and culture, and these things help determine the success of your company. People are drawn to people with integrity and conviction. People shy away from inconsistency and indecisiveness. This is true for people and companies.


  3. Keep your pitch simple, stupid, and know your audience.

    There’s nothing more important than getting your pitch right when you’re in the early stages of a startup. These are the lines that explain who you are, what value you’re bringing to your customers, why they’re going to love it, and when you’re going to prove it. Like a resume catered to different roles, a pitch may need to be crafted for different people. For example, if you have a high-tech product and are pitching to a room full of investors with little to no technological background, your pitch may include less technical details than it would when given to a room full of developers. In either scenario, don’t overdo it. Make sure there can be no doubt about what your product does and why it will be a success.


  4. Never stop getting to know your customer.

    Yes, you’ve got to do some research up front to make sure the people who like your photo app are really females in their late 20s-30s and not males in their 40s-50s, but you can’t stop there. You have to continue putting in the time to understand your base so that you don’t miss the trends or the opportunities for new value.


  5. Don’t be afraid to ask for help, and don’t forget to repay your debts.

    Your journey isn’t going to be smooth sailing. You’re going to encounter roadblocks that you can’t solve, and you’re going to have to reach out for help. It’s absolutely fine to reach out for help, but don’t forget to say thank you - immediately and then once more when your head is above water.
